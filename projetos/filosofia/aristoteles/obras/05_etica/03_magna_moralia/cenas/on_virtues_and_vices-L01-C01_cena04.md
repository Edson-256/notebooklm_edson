7  To  profligacy  belongs  choosing  harmful  and  base 
pleasures  and  enjoyments,  and  thinking  that  the 
happiest  people  are  those  who  pass  their  lives  in 
pleasures  of  that  kind,  and  being  fond  of  laughter  and 
mockery  and  jokes  and  levity  in  words  and  deeds. 

8  Profligacy  is  accompanied  by  disorder,  shamelessness, 
irregularity,  luxury,  slackness,  carelessness,  negli- 
gence, remissness. 

9  To  uncontrol  it  belongs  to  choose  the  enjoyment  of 
pleasures  when  reason  would  restrain,  and  although 
one  believes  that  it  would  be  better  not  to  participate 
in  them,  to  participate  in  them  all  the  same,  and 
while  thinking  one  ought  to  do  fine  and  expedient 
things  yet  to  abstain  from  them  for  the  sake  of  one's 

10  pleasures.  The  concomitants  of  uncontrol  are  soft- 
ness and  negligence  and  in  general  the  same  as  those 
of  profligacy.

7  Of  meanness  there  are  three  kinds,  love  of  base 

8  gain,  parsimony,  niggardliness.  Love  of  base  gain 
makes  men  seek  profit  from  all  sources  and  pay  more 

9  regard  to  the  profit  than  to  the  disgrace  ;  parsimony 
makes  them  unwilling  to  spend  money  on  a  necessary 

10  object  ;  niggardliness  causes  them  only  to  spend  in 
driblets  and  in  a  bad  way,  and  to  lose  more  than 
they  gain  by  not  at  the  proper  moment  letting  go 

11  the  difference.  It  belongs  to  meanness  to  set  a 
very  high  value  on  money  and  to  think  nothing 
that  brings  profit  a  disgrace — a  menial  and  servile 
and  squalid  mode  of  life,  alien  to  ambition  and  to 

12  liberality.  Meanness  is  accompanied  by  pettiness, 
sulkiness,  self-abasement,  lack  of  proportion,  ignoble- 
ness,  misanthropy.
