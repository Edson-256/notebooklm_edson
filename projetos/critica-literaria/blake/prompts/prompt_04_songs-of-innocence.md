## Body — cover, in pt-BR, roughly this arc
1. **O estado, não a idade:** Innocence is a **state of the soul** (= Beulah, the lower paradise — cross-reference unit 2), a **way of seeing**, not a childhood age that is later lost. The crucial, counter-intuitive point: Innocence is NOT naive bliss — it is the world as the child / unfallen imagination sees it, and it **already contains its own shadow** (a social cruelty it can sense but cannot yet name).
2. **A gênese das canções:** the **Introduction** — the piper, the child on the cloud asking for a song about a Lamb, the song moving from breath to book. The child = Innocence dictating to the artist; the first thing asked for is the Lamb, the volume's central symbol.
3. **O criador manso:** **The Lamb** ("Little Lamb who made thee") — Innocence's vision of a gentle, child-made creator (the Lamb-Christ), with no distance between God, child and creature. Tie to Frye's contrast (ch. 6) between the Bible's "loving Father" read poetically and the cold, abstract God of "natural religion".
4. **A forma humana divina:** **The Divine Image** — "Mercy, Pity, Peace, and Love" are at once God and man; the divine has a human face ("the human form divine"). Tie to Blake's "All deities reside in the human breast" — the humanist/imaginative reading of religion Frye foregrounds.
5. **A textura de Beulah:** **The Echoing Green** (a whole day of Innocence, ending on the "darkening Green" — the night is already in it), **Night** (angels guard the nests; the lion lies down with the lamb; how Innocence *resolves* the shadow — redemption, not denial), **Infant Joy** ("Joy is my name" — joy as Innocence's proper name).
6. **Onde a sombra entra (o coração do áudio):** **Holy Thursday** of Innocence (the charity children — the voice admires the "wise guardians of the poor" yet the scene is institutionalised poverty; Innocence registers the injustice but cannot accuse it) and **The Chimney Sweeper** of Innocence (the father who *sold* the child; Tom Dacre's dream of the Angel; the planted irony of "So if all do their duty, they need not fear harm" — the child repeats the morality that keeps her docile, and by believing it, unwittingly indicts the whole machine). Make explicit Blake's method: Innocence tells a truth it does not know it is telling; the listener sees the trap the child cannot.
7. **The Little Black Boy:** Innocence trying to dissolve racism into spiritual equality ("but a cloud"), yet carrying the wound it tries to heal — again the pattern of the shadow Innocence cannot name.
8. **A lente de Frye (3 pontos):** (a) Blake is **no literary freak** — the simple Songs sit on a whole visionary tradition (the Renaissance humanists; the Hebraic-prophetic attack on "the rich grinding the faces of the poor"; the "age of Blake"), so their simplicity is deceptive; Frye notes Blake worked an innocence/experience antithesis from the *Poetical Sketches* on. (b) Innocence is what the imagination fights to **recover** — "liberty… the effort of the imagination to recover the state of innocence" — so Beulah is a goal, not mere nostalgia (and Orc/Urizen are born "out of his symbols of innocence and experience"). (c) The God of Innocence is the Bible's "loving Father" read as **vision**, not the abstract legislator of natural religion.
9. **Para levar:** invite the listener, when he opens the book in English, to read each song asking two things at once: "what does this celebrate — and where, under the sweetness, does the shadow it cannot yet name flicker?" Set up the contrast with **Experience** (unit 5) and the **pairing of the two contrary states** (unit 6): one does not return to naive Innocence — one passes *through* Experience toward a higher, organised Innocence.
