## Body — cover, in pt-BR, roughly this arc
1. **What *Jerusalem* is, and why it is the summit:** Blake's longest, last prophecy (4 chapters, 100 plates, ~4000 lines). With *Milton* it forms a single epic: *Milton* = the poet attaining the vision; *Jerusalem* = that vision expounded in terms of all humanity (Frye). Unpack the title: **Jerusalem is the Emanation of the Giant Albion** — Albion = the total Man / humanity / England / the listener; his Emanation, separated at the Fall, is Jerusalem = liberty, brotherhood, the city of imagination. The whole poem is the drama of a sleeping man who has cast out his own liberty.
2. **The four-act structure (Frye, ch. 11):** the four chapters = Fall / the world under the law / the coming of Jesus and the resistance to him / the apocalypse-awakening — the Bible's own arc (Creation–Fall–Redemption–Apocalypse) rewritten. Each chapter addressed to a public; chapter 4 "To the Christians." Give the listener this spine of four so he can navigate.
3. **Chapter 1 — Albion falls asleep and casts out Jerusalem:** Albion turns away from the Divine Vision, enters the "Void, outside of Existence", falls into Selfhood, and banishes Jerusalem and Jesus from himself ("Where hast thou hidden thy Emanation lovely Jerusalem"). **Vala/Babylon** — the separated Emanation become a veiled nature-goddess, the veil of "nature" and moral law, later revealed as Rahab — reigns over the fallen world.
4. **Los and Golgonooza (the secret hero):** while Albion sleeps and Vala reigns, **Los labours all night at his furnaces**, refusing despair, building **Golgonooza, the city of art** — and wrestling/subduing his own **Spectre** (the auto-aniquilação of unit 12, now a nightly labour). For Frye (ch. 11), Golgonooza is the **City of God**: art is what holds human form alive inside the catastrophe so the world does not dissolve into the Void. Note the repeated word **"fourfold"** — flag it for the climax.
5. **The fourfold vision:** Blake's four degrees of sight = the four levels of unit 2 seen as ways of *seeing* — **Single vision** (dead matter, Locke/Newton, Ulro) / twofold / threefold (Beulah) / **fourfold** (Eden, the full human vision where everything is living Human Form). The motto **"May God us keep / From Single vision & Newtons sleep"** is the poem's spirit — but state clearly it comes from a **letter (1802), not from *Jerusalem* itself.** The enemy throughout is single vision; the poem fights to open the eye from one dimension to four.
6. **"To the Christians" — the golden string:** quote the great quatrain — "I give you the end of a golden string, / Only wind it into a ball, / It will lead you in at Heavens gate, / Built in Jerusalems wall." Explain: the poem is the string; to read it patiently is to be led to the gate. Tie to Blake's prose — "to Labour in Knowledge, is to Build up Jerusalem" — and to Frye's own closing image (ch. 12): when we grasp that in Blake there are no accidents, only one comprehensible form, "we have wound up all of his golden string and are standing in front of his gate."
7. **THE CLIMAX — Albion awakes:** after the long night, the dawn. "The Breath Divine went forth over the morning hills **Albion rose**." He rises in flames, takes his **fourfold** Bow, and — like Los — throws himself into the furnaces. Then the miracle that resolves the whole myth: **"the Furnaces became / Fountains of Living Waters"** — the furnaces become fountains; Los's hard night-labour is revealed as living water. **The four Zoas reunite** ("Urizen & Luvah & Tharmas & Urthona arose into Albions Bosom"), each restored to his place. Albion stands "Fourfold" before Jesus, and calls "Awake! Awake Jerusalem!" — the apocalyptic marriage of Albion and Jerusalem. The final plate: **"All Human Forms identified"** — tree, metal, earth, stone — external nature revealed at last as the body of the human imagination; no more "outside"; the myth closes.
8. **Frye's two chapters (the lens):** (a) **ch. 11, "The City of God"** — *Jerusalem* as the Bible recreated in English terms (Canaan over England, London as the fallen Jerusalem, Tyburn as Calvary), Golgonooza as the City of God; the harsh "age of Goya and Beethoven" tone, and the deliberately **suppressed climax** flashing only on the last plates. (b) **ch. 12, "The Burden of the Valley of Vision"** — with *Jerusalem* the canon is closed; Blake turns to painting; the fresco campaign and its cruel reception ("an unfortunate lunatic"); Blake's startling **mental independence**; and the book's closing thesis — an **"iconography of the imagination"**: learning to read Blake makes you able to read the Bible, Milton, Spenser, Keats — all literature — anew, because all symbolism is mutually intelligible (foreshadow *Anatomy of Criticism*). The system serves **reading, not worship**.
9. **Para levar + the bridge to Frye:** this is the summit and the **end of the arc**. Tell the listener how to actually read *Jerusalem* in English — NOT cover to cover at once, but: take the golden string and wind it (read each chapter's manifesto + lyric, then the great passages, esp. the awakening on plates ~95–99), let the four-act structure orient him, and return to the unit-2 map when a strange name appears. Then close the whole companion: he is no longer facing an "incomprehensible" author — he has the map, the poems, and the great passages in hand. **"Agora você está pronto para o *Fearful Symmetry* de Frye"** — the whole book, cover to cover. Everything in this series was a lens Frye ground; now it is time to read the master in the original. Pick up the book, find the golden string in it, and wind it to the gate.
