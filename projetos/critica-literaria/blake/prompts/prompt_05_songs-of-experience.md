## Body — cover, in pt-BR, roughly this arc
1. **A moldura:** the 1794 volume and its subtitle "the two contrary states of the human soul"; Innocence and Experience as two **ways of seeing the same world**, not good vs evil nor before vs after. Tie to Frye's core claim that **perception is creative** — Experience is the world seen by the **fallen** imagination that has accepted an external, law-bound "nature" (the embryo of Urizen / "natural religion").
2. **A voz do Bardo:** the "Introduction" and "Earth's Answer" — the prophetic voice calling the imprisoned Earth to wake. Explain why Frye hears in this Bard the same prophetic imagination of the later major prophecies.
3. **Os poemas-chave** (explain, don't recite): **The Tyger** (energy/wrath the Lamb cannot see; the unanswered "Did he who made the Lamb make thee?"; the smith = Los forging); **London** ("charter'd", "mind-forg'd manacles" — the mental, and therefore undoable, prison); **The Sick Rose** (repressed/hidden desire rotting); **The Chimney Sweeper** and **Holy Thursday** of Experience (religion and power building heaven out of the misery of the small); **The Garden of Love / A Poison Tree** ("Thou shalt not"; hidden wrath becoming poison).
4. **A lente de Frye (3 pontos):** (a) Innocence/Experience are **states**, not stages or people — Experience is the night before a possible higher, "organized" Innocence; (b) the villain is **abstraction** placed above life (the embryonic Urizen); (c) **energy is holy** — what fallen morality calls "evil" is dammed-up divine energy (foreshadow the *Marriage of Heaven and Hell*).
5. **Para levar:** invite the listener, when he opens the book in English, to read each poem asking "what prison is this a portrait of — and where in the poem does the exit flicker?"
