Warm, vivid, conversational — a great teacher talking, not a lecture.

## Output language

## Two sources you are given
1. **Blake — *Songs of Innocence* and *Songs of Experience*** (the primary texts being explained — you need BOTH, because this unit reads the contrary pairs side by side).

## What this audio IS and IS NOT
- It is an **explanation / guide**, NOT a reading. **Do NOT recite or translate the poems line by line** — the listener reads the verses himself in English. Quote at most a short phrase in English when you need to anchor a point (e.g. "Did he who made the Lamb make thee?", "Without Contraries is no progression", "Cruelty has a Human Heart"), then explain it in pt-BR.
- Ground your interpretation in **Frye's actual reading** (source 2). When you say "para Frye…", make sure it reflects what the Frye chapter actually argues — the doctrine of contraries, evil as negative (self-restraint or restraint of others), "Man is a twofold being", and that "Mercy, Pity, Peace and Love" are the attributes of unfallen Man (Frye himself pairs "The Human Abstract" and "The Divine Image"). **Do not invent positions or quotations.** If something is your own gloss rather than Frye's, present it as such.
- Name Frye explicitly — this audio is openly a companion to Frye. (This is the opposite of hiding the method.)

## Body — cover, in pt-BR, roughly this arc
1. **A ideia que organiza tudo:** "contrário" is NOT the same as "moral opposite". Innocence and Experience are **two contrary states of the human soul**, two ways of seeing the same world — not good vs evil, not before vs after. The book is built as a **machine of pairs**: many Innocence poems have a bitter twin in Experience, and the meaning lives in the **space between them**. Tie to Blake's "Without Contraries is no progression" (from the *Marriage*), which Frye puts at the center of this chapter.
2. **Os pares, lidos frente a frente** (explain the contrast, don't recite): **The Lamb / The Tyger** (meekness vs fiery energy; the deliberately unanswered "Did he who made the Lamb make thee?"; the smith = Los forging); **the two Holy Thursdays** (charity as beauty / "multitudes of lambs" vs charity unmasked as cruelty / "Babes reduced to misery"); **the two Chimney Sweepers** (Tom's dream of the Angel who frees the children vs the child in the snow who accuses "God & his Priest & King, / Who make up a heaven of our misery"); **the two Nurse's Songs** (play freely allowed vs play soured by the watching, envious adult); **Infant Joy / Infant Sorrow** (arrival as joy vs as struggle and revolt — "like a fiend hid in a cloud"); **The Divine Image / A Divine Image** ("the human form divine", Mercy-Pity-Peace-Love, vs "Cruelty has a Human Heart", "the human form a fiery forge"). Make the brilliant point that the Experience poem reuses the EXACT vocabulary of its twin to say the opposite — one human form, two states, not two substances.
3. **A lente de Frye (cap. 3, "Beyond Good and Evil"), ~4 pontos:** (a) man is "twofold", and the friction of contraries is what moves life — "Without Contraries is no progression"; (b) evil is **negative** — there is no evil act, "all evil consists either in self-restraint or restraint of others"; so the Tyger's energy is holy and the cruelty of "A Divine Image" is dammed-up life turned against itself; (c) "Mercy, Pity, Peace and Love" do NOT depend on evil — Frye explicitly pairs "The Human Abstract" and "The Divine Image" and says "destroy the evil, and the four virtues will be seen to be the attributes of unfallen Man"; (d) going "beyond good and evil" means refusing to freeze either state into a moral absolute — eating the tree of the knowledge of good and evil is the tree of death; the goal is the **higher, "organized" Innocence** reached only THROUGH Experience, not by retreating from it.
4. **Para levar:** invite the listener, when he opens the book in English, to read in **pairs** — for each Innocence poem, find its twin in Experience and read them in one sitting, asking "what does each see that the other can't — and what do only the two together reveal?" Don't elect the "right side": the soul needs both, and the destination is not the childhood garden but the organized Innocence on the far side of Experience. That is why the book has two halves: because man is "twofold".
