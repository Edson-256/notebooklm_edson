## What this unit is
This is an **orientation unit** — the first of the companion. It tells **Blake's life**, to prepare the listener for everything that follows (the mythological system, the *Songs*, the prophecies, and Frye's book). **There is NO Frye chapter for this unit.** Do not pretend to quote or paraphrase Frye here; the only sources are the two biographies.

## Two sources you are given
1. **Irene Langridge, *William Blake*** (a biography).
2. **Charles Gardner, *William Blake*** (a biography).
Both draw, in turn, on Gilchrist's *Life*. You ground everything you say in these two biographical texts.

## What this audio IS and IS NOT
- It is a **narrated orientation to Blake's life and character**, NOT a reading of his poems. Quote at most a short phrase in English when it anchors a point (e.g. "they are not mine"), then explain in pt-BR.
- **Anti-invention is the central constraint.** Do NOT invent dates, anecdotes, names, or quotations. State only what the two biographies actually report. If a detail is uncertain or the sources disagree, say so briefly or omit it. This listener's domain prizes factual fidelity above color.
  - Concretely: the sources give these anchors — born London, 28 Broad Street, 1757; childhood visions (God at the window at age four; the tree full of angels at Peckham Rye; the father who nearly thrashed him for it); drawing school under Pars; apprenticeship to the engraver **Basire**; drawing the Gothic tombs in **Westminster Abbey** (which shaped his style); friction at the **Royal Academy** under Moser and with **Reynolds**; the failed first paint-shop with Parker; marriage to **Catherine Boucher** (1782), whom he taught to read, write, draw, engrave and colour, and who became his lifelong partner and workshop assistant; the death of his beloved brother **Robert**, whose spirit (in a vision) is said to have revealed the **illuminated-printing / relief-etching** method; lifelong **poverty and obscurity**; the loyal buyer **Butts**; the **Felpham** years (1800–1803) under the patron **Hayley**, which chafed him ("Do be my enemy for friendship's sake"); the **1803 sedition trial** (the drunken soldier **Schofield**; accused of cursing the King; **acquitted**, with Hayley testifying for him); the last years at **Fountain Court**; the late disciples around **John Linnell** and **Samuel Palmer** (the "House of the Interpreter"); learning **Italian at 67** to read **Dante**; the **Job** engravings as the crowning work; and **death on a Sunday in August 1827, singing songs to his Maker** ("they are not mine").
  - NOTE: the label "the Ancients" does NOT appear in these two sources. If you mention it at all, flag it as a later name, not something the biographies say.

## Body — cover, in pt-BR, roughly this arc
1. **Nascimento e a infância das visões** (Londres, 1757; o menino solitário; Deus na janela aos quatro; a árvore de anjos em Peckham Rye; o pai que quase o surra). Frame the key point: for Blake, vision was not metaphor — he *literally saw*, and held the imaginative world more real than the "vegetable" one. This is the root of everything.
2. **O aprendizado** (Pars; Basire; above all the Gothic tombs of **Westminster Abbey** that formed his style and his love of firm outline). Explain why the craftsman's training matters.
3. **O atrito com a Royal Academy e Reynolds** (the "not even begun" outburst; genius as flame vs. genius as taught precept). Frame Blake as the dissenter from official art.
4. **Catherine** (taught to read and to print; lifelong partner and co-worker; without her, no illuminated books).
5. **A invenção do método** — relief etching / illuminated printing, said to be revealed by the dead brother **Robert** in a vision; Blake as author + illustrator + engraver + printer + publisher in one. Stress: for Blake the engraved, hand-coloured page is inseparable from the poem.
6. **Pobreza e os anos de Felpham sob Hayley** — patronage as a chain; the break of 1803; "liberty and poverty".
7. **O julgamento por sedição (1803)** — Schofield; acquittal; Hayley's help; the mark it left.
8. **Os últimos anos** — Fountain Court; Linnell and the young disciples (Palmer; "House of the Interpreter"); learning Italian at 67 for Dante; the **Job** engravings as the crowning achievement.
9. **A morte, cantando (1827)** — the Sunday in August; the songs to his Maker; "they are not mine"; the last sketch of Catherine.

## Closing — "Para levar" (the lens this unit gives)
Tie the life to the reading. Three takeaways: (a) **he literally saw** — the visionary premise that perception is creative (foreshadow Frye without quoting him); (b) **he was craftsman as much as poet** — the illuminated page is half the work, so reading Blake as bare text loses half; (c) **he was the dissenter** — against Reynolds, against Hayley, against the sedition charge, against "decent" religion — and that refusal arms the rebellious energy of his poetry. End by pointing forward: with the man known, the next unit gives the **system** (Albion, the Four Zoas, Urizen, Los) that makes the work legible.
